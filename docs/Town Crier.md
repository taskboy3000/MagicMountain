A **Town Crier / Daily Broadside / Market Bulletin** is an excellent mechanism for influencing buyer choice through narrative rather than exposed faction meters.

It should not replace the mechanical value of standing and future opportunities. But it can make the player _care_ which buyer receives an artifact.

> The player sees what factions are doing to the settlement, then decides whether today’s payout is worth feeding that faction more influence.

That directly connects world narration to the salvage-and-sell loop.

# Why This Works

A player choosing among buyers has several possible motives:

|Motivation|Mechanism|
|---|---|
|Immediate profit|Offer price|
|Future access|Faction standing and commissions|
|Seasonal influence|Which faction gains artifacts|
|Emotional reaction|Daily news about what factions have recently done|

The Town Crier provides the fourth element. It allows a player to say:

> “The Syndicate is paying best, but after reading what happened at the sleeping hall, I don’t feel like selling them another heater today.”

Or:

> “I dislike the Purifiers, but after three cookhouse fires, their offer to safely remove this unstable device suddenly seems reasonable.”

That is not forced morality. It is informed opportunism mixed with human reaction.

# The Town Crier Should Not Be Objective

I would avoid a neutral omniscient newspaper that states faction consequences as facts from the game designer.

Instead, make this an in-world communication channel with its own character and limitations:

- announcements;
    
- gossip;
    
- notices;
    
- market complaints;
    
- legal postings;
    
- sermon excerpts;
    
- editorial remarks;
    
- overheard rumors;
    
- corrections;
    
- public accusations;
    
- small classified advertisements.
    

The player should receive **evidence of the social world**, not a morality report.

## Possible Presentation Names

|Name|Feel|
|---|---|
|**The Crier**|Immediate, oral, temporary boomtown|
|**Camp Bulletin**|Practical, settlement-like|
|**Mountain Broadside**|Characterful, printable, satirical|
|**Market Bell**|Commerce-oriented|
|**Notices from the Slopes**|More atmospheric|
|**The Temporary Record**|Funny and slightly Faculty-adjacent|

I like **The Crier** as the mechanic, even if the presentation includes handbills, shouted announcements, and posted notices rather than one literal person shouting all day.

# What the Crier Reports

The Crier can publish short narrative fragments based on:

- dominant faction influence;
    
- recent artifact dispositions;
    
- significant artifact sales;
    
- the season environment;
    
- faction conflicts;
    
- consequences of prior events;
    
- minor absurdities generated by the settlement.
    

Most of these should be **free events**: they add context but do not consume player turns.

## Example: Syndicate Influence

> **Market Notice:** The Syndicate has opened three new warming stalls along the north road. Customers may rent heat by the quarter-hour. Blankets remain available to those qualifying for credit.

A player holding a heating artifact now understands what selling to the Syndicate might reinforce.

## Example: LibreMount Influence

> **Public Notice, amended twice:** LibreMount volunteers installed four heating units in the sleeping hall last night. All four function. Only one requires someone to remain awake beside it.

The player may admire the result, distrust the process, or both.

## Example: Faculty Influence

> **Faculty Correction:** The dark annex does not contain “hidden lamps,” as alleged. It contains restricted luminous specimens. Unauthorized staring remains prohibited.

A player with a light- or signal-producing artifact now has an opinion about where it may end up.

## Example: Purifier Influence

> **Safety Posting:** Two devices surrendered this week were found unsuitable for household use. One household has filed a complaint that it was much colder after becoming safe.

## Example: Revelationist Influence

> **Pilgrim Advisory:** The warm device at the lower shrine is no longer accepting offerings of wax. The wax melts. This has not reduced offerings.

# Narrative Cues Should Be Mixed, Not Propaganda Meters

A major strength of this system is that the player should not always receive a simple negative or positive story about a faction.

Each faction’s news should show both usefulness and cost.

|Faction|Positive Crier Story|Troubling Crier Story|
|---|---|---|
|Revelationists|Hostels distribute meals and warmth to new arrivals.|A useful device is removed from practical service after being declared sacred.|
|Purifiers|Inspectors identify a dangerous household device before it harms anyone.|Families lose useful equipment through overzealous confiscation.|
|Faculty|Researchers discover a repeated dangerous behavior in common artifacts.|Needed artifacts disappear into restricted collections.|
|Syndicate|Traders get food, shelter, and heating devices distributed rapidly.|Essential services acquire prices, debts, and tiers of access.|
|LibreMount|Activists open hoarded supplies to excluded households.|Untested artifacts circulate without inspection or responsibility.|

This is important because the player should not be trained:

> “Bad news about faction X means never sell to faction X.”

Instead, the player should continually face discomfort:

> “I see why people support them. I also see what they are becoming.”

# The Most Powerful Version: Report Consequences of Player Sales

General faction color is useful. But the feature becomes emotionally powerful when it occasionally reflects something the player personally did.

A player sells a valuable heater to the Syndicate.

Later:

> **Market Report:** A premium warming booth has opened beside the eastern dormitory. The unit inside bears a repaired seam you find uncomfortably familiar.

A player sells a water device to LibreMount.

Later:

> **Public Notice:** Drinking water is now available without charge beside the lower gate. Visitors are advised not to comment on the taste until LibreMount agrees whether taste constitutes regulation.

A player sells an anomalous panel to the Faculty.

Later:

> **Faculty Advisory:** Residents near the restricted annex should disregard repeated tones after dark. Any written descriptions of those tones are to be delivered unopened.

A player surrenders an unstable medical-looking artifact to Purifiers.

Later:

> **Complaint Posted Outside the Inspection Yard:** The device may have been unsafe. It also may have been the only thing helping her sleep. Both statements may be true.

A player sells a responsive artifact to Revelationists.

Later:

> **Pilgrim Report:** The new relic gives no sign today. Several witnesses insist its silence is more significant than yesterday’s light.

That is excellent feedback: the player sees that an ordinary sale entered the social fabric.

# Do Not Make Every Sale Echo

This must remain occasional. If every artifact generates a follow-up article, the system becomes predictable and exhausting.

I would reserve explicit sale echoes for:

- high-value artifacts;
    
- artifacts with revealed secondary behavior;
    
- artifacts sold during urgent environmental conditions;
    
- artifacts involved in a contested sale;
    
- sales that move a player into “recognized” or “entangled” faction standing.
    

Ordinary sales can still contribute invisibly to faction influence and sometimes appear in aggregate stories.

# The Crier as a Source of Buyer Motivation

This becomes especially useful if a daily Crier item establishes current demand before the player starts prospecting.

## Example: Cold Snap

> **Morning Crier:** The sleeping halls have begun burning broken furniture. The Syndicate claims heating inventory will arrive by afternoon. LibreMount says it already arrived and is being stored behind a lock.

Later, the player salvages a heating artifact and sees:

|Buyer|Offer|Player Context|
|---|--:|---|
|Syndicate|26|High offer, but the player has heard the hoarding accusation.|
|LibreMount|22|Lower offer, but may immediately heat the sleeping halls.|
|Purifiers|18|Device has shown some instability; surrender avoids risk to residents.|

This is much more compelling than bare offer values.

# The Crier Can Also Be Wrong

A further refinement: reports should sometimes be incomplete, biased, or disputed.

Not randomly dishonest in a way that punishes the player, but socially unreliable in a way that fits the world.

Examples:

> **Correction:** Yesterday’s report that the Faculty had secured six functioning water devices was inaccurate. The Faculty states that three are not water devices and the remaining three are not functioning in any publicly relevant sense.

Or:

> LibreMount denies responsibility for opening the warehouse. The Syndicate denies the warehouse contained essential supplies. Families carrying Syndicate-marked heaters decline to comment.

Or:

> A Purifier statement attributes last night’s glow to hazardous discharge. Revelationist speakers attribute it to answered prayer. The Syndicate has begun selling tinted viewing lenses.

This is exactly your tone: practical, dry, and socially revealing.

# Mechanical Role of the Crier

The Crier should remain primarily narrative, but it can be tied to actual state:

|Input|Output|
|---|---|
|Faction influence rising|More faction-visible stories|
|Important disposition|Possible later echo|
|Seasonal environment|Stories about current needs or harms|
|Active commission|Story that explains why a faction wants a behavior|
|Faction conflict|Contradictory reports or public disputes|

## What it should not do

The Crier should not:

- explicitly announce hidden faction influence points;
    
- state which buyer is strategically correct;
    
- give mechanical buffs simply for reading;
    
- consume a turn;
    
- become a mandatory wall of text before play.
    

# Minimal Implementable Version

This feature can be added incrementally.

## Phase 1: Daily Crier Item

At day start or login:

- display one short Crier story;
    
- choose it from YAML based on broad faction influence or current season environment;
    
- no mechanical effect;
    
- no turn consumed.
    

## Phase 2: Sale Echoes

For significant artifact dispositions:

- save an echo hook in player/season state;
    
- later display a faction-colored report referencing the disposition.
    

## Phase 3: Crier-Supported Requests

Use the Crier to announce active buyer needs:

> The Faculty is offering unusual terms for devices that repeat patterns under load.

This makes commissions feel like events in the world rather than UI quests.

# Suggested Design Principle

Add this to the faction/salvage integration document:

```markdown
## The Crier

The Crier is an in-world narrative channel that reports on life around the Mountain.

It may present:
- faction activity
- community benefits
- abuses and scandals
- market conditions
- faction conflicts
- disputed interpretations
- echoes of significant player artifact sales
- temporary faction requests or demand

The Crier exists to help the player understand the society their transactions are shaping.

Crier items:
- do not consume turns
- are short and skippable
- are grounded in current season state
- may be incomplete, biased, or disputed
- should show consequences without declaring moral conclusions

The Crier may influence player buyer choices through context and emotional reaction, but it does not directly instruct the player which faction to support.
```

# Bottom Line

Yes: the Crier is a strong narrative counterweight to simply choosing the highest offer.

It gives players a reason to see buyer choice as more than price comparison:

> “I know what these people have been doing with the things we sell them.”

Combined with standing-based opportunities, this yields the right structure:

| System           | Motivation                                                       |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Offer price      | Immediate competitive gain                                       |
| Faction standing | Future access and entanglement                                   |
| The Crier        | Narrative knowledge and emotional consequence                    |
| Season recap     | Meaning assigned after the player’s practical choices accumulate |

That is not a morality system. It is a system in which the player can no longer pretend that selling an artifact ends at the cash register.
