The world ended a long time ago, if it ended at all. Nobody alive remembers it, and most people do not spend much time thinking about it. They have towns, markets, congregations, creditors, local feuds, bad weather, and opinions about strangers. The old machines are simply part of the landscape: useful when they work, dangerous when they do not, and generally better left to people willing to lose a hand for profit.

Then the Mountain appears, and ordinary life becomes briefly unreasonable.

It is not a legend, a vision, or a miracle, although people describe it as all three. It is physically present: immense, artificial-looking in places, geological-looking in others, riddled with chambers, debris, devices, hazards, and mechanisms that appear to serve purposes no living person understands.

It remains for a short time—approximately one season of play—and then it vanishes.

Later, elsewhere, it appears again.

Every appearance creates a boomtown. Prospectors, traders, preachers, scavengers, scholars, guards, gamblers, frauds, refugees, technicians, and pilgrims rush toward it before the opportunity disappears.

The player is one of those opportunists.

Not a hero. Not a savior. Not even a believer.

Just someone trying to make a living—and perhaps a fortune—before the Mountain leaves.

---

# What the Mountain Means

Nobody in the game knows what the Mountain actually is.

As designer, you know one essential truth:

> The Mountain is material. It was made by people. Its apparent magic is misunderstood technology.

That truth is never announced to the player. The player receives fragments:

- a sealed box that emits heat;
    
- a strip of metal that stiffens when moved;
    
- a panel that displays patterns no one can interpret;
    
- a device that appears useful as a water purifier until it does something unrelated and disturbing;
    
- artifacts whose most marketable function may be a crude misuse of their original purpose.
    

The people around the Mountain construct explanations around these fragments. Some explanations are sincere. Some are profitable. Some are fearful. Some are nearly right for completely wrong reasons.

The Mountain is not the moral actor in the story. It is the pressure placed upon society.

# The Temporary Settlement

Every season forms a new settlement around the Mountain.

It is part gold rush, part refugee camp, part pilgrimage site, part black market, part academic expedition, and part carnival built by people who would be insulted to hear it described as a carnival.

It contains:

- makeshift exchanges where artifacts are bought and sold;
    
- repair stalls and salvage yards;
    
- shrines assembled from scrap and rumor;
    
- inspection stations and confiscation pits;
    
- cookhouses, clinics, tents, guarded warehouses, and illicit markets;
    
- notice boards offering contracts;
    
- scorekeepers, gossip, accusations, and sudden wealth.
    

Players do not need to own a specific business. They are prospectors and operators: they extract artifacts, force them into useful forms, and sell them into whatever society has formed this season.

A device is valuable not because the player knows what it is, but because they can make it do something somebody wants.

# Seasonal Worlds

The Mountain never appears in a neutral location.

Each season has an environmental circumstance that shapes life around it:

- drought;
    
- severe cold;
    
- agricultural abundance;
    
- insect swarms;
    
- wildfire conditions;
    
- dust storms;
    
- mass migration;
    
- inexplicable animal behavior;
    
- failing water systems;
    
- a region already strained by conflict or scarcity.
    

These conditions do not hand a faction a statistical advantage. They create needs, fears, temptations, and interpretations.

A heat-producing artifact is valuable in a freezing settlement. In a tropical floodplain, perhaps it is worthless until someone discovers it can sterilize water. In a Purifier-dominated market, a weapon-like artifact may be difficult to sell openly—but exceptionally profitable to the wrong customer.

Every season asks:

> What will people value here, under these conditions, while the Mountain is still present?

# The Factions

The factions are not player classes, moral alignments, or teams that offer direct buffs.

They are competing explanations of what the Mountain means and what should be done about it. Players are not primarily trying to serve them. Players encounter them, exploit them, offend them, accept their contracts, sell into their markets, and live in the world their influence creates.

Names remain provisional, but the five-faction structure is strong because it is unstable: no single worldview naturally balances all the others.

---

## 1. The Revelationists

### What they believe

The Mountain is a visitation: a gift, warning, test, or instrument of salvation. Its arrival has purpose. Its artifacts are signs as much as objects.

Some Revelationists are compassionate believers offering shelter, food, and meaning in a frightened settlement. Some are charismatic grifters selling blessings and relic certificates. Some are severe people who conclude that those obstructing the Mountain’s purpose must be corrected.

### What they protect

- meaning;
    
- community;
    
- hope;
    
- ritual;
    
- moral order.
    

### When they gain influence

The settlement becomes more pious and more organized around belief:

- pilgrims arrive;
    
- shrines appear;
    
- artifacts become relics;
    
- charitable aid networks grow;
    
- testimony and prophecy circulate.
    

But also:

- dissent becomes suspicious;
    
- non-believers face social pressure;
    
- competing interpretations provoke conflict;
    
- dangerous devices may be revered rather than understood.
    

### Their relationship to the player

They may value an artifact because of the story attached to it rather than its utility. A blinking device might be a sign. A machine that produces clean water might become a holy object. The player can profit from that demand without ever believing in it.

---

## 2. The Archivists

### What they believe

The Mountain is evidence. Whatever it is, humanity must study it before destroying, selling, worshipping, or misusing it.

They collect devices, fragments, observations, diagrams, failed experiments, contradictory accounts, and anything that might eventually produce understanding.

### What they protect

- knowledge;
    
- memory;
    
- investigation;
    
- preservation;
    
- long-term understanding.
    

### When they gain influence

The settlement becomes more orderly and intellectually active:

- laboratories and cataloging tents appear;
    
- more artifacts are preserved for study;
    
- technical vocabulary enters common speech;
    
- practical improvements may emerge from accumulated knowledge.
    

But also:

- useful technology may sit in storage while committees debate;
    
- urgent military or material needs may be neglected;
    
- scholars may become a privileged class;
    
- the settlement may understand its dangers better without defending itself against them.
    

### Their relationship to the player

They may pay well for artifacts exhibiting unexpected secondary behavior, incomplete patterns, or evidence of complex function. A device that is merely a profitable heater to another buyer may become far more valuable once it begins producing structured output.

---

## 3. The Syndicate

### What they believe

The Mountain is an opportunity. Debating its meaning is a luxury for people who already ate today.

Anything useful should be sold. Anything desirable should be priced. Anything scarce should have an owner. If the Mountain can create prosperity, failing to exploit it is the true sin.

### What they protect

- trade;
    
- liquidity;
    
- ambition;
    
- material prosperity;
    
- personal advancement.
    

### When they gain influence

The settlement becomes lively, wealthy, and commercially efficient:

- exchanges expand;
    
- buyers are easier to find;
    
- services appear rapidly;
    
- artifact markets become sophisticated;
    
- people with nerve can rise quickly.
    

But also:

- fraud proliferates;
    
- monopolies form;
    
- desperate people enter ruinous bargains;
    
- poverty becomes more visible beside wealth;
    
- everything, including belief and safety, acquires a price.
    

### Their relationship to the player

They are the easiest faction to understand: if a device can be sold, they want it sold. But they also create price bubbles, counterfeit markets, shady buyers, and temptations to push an artifact beyond what prudence permits.

---

## 4. The Purifiers

### What they believe

The old world destroyed itself with things it insisted were progress. The Mountain is not a blessing or a resource deposit. It is a recurring contamination event.

Not every recovered device should be repaired. Some things should be buried, broken, burned, or never allowed into the hands of people desperate enough to use them.

### What they protect

- safety;
    
- restraint;
    
- containment;
    
- skepticism;
    
- survival through refusal.
    

### When they gain influence

The settlement becomes more cautious:

- inspections appear;
    
- hazardous artifacts are removed;
    
- accidental disasters decline;
    
- risky markets shrink;
    
- people begin asking whether an artifact should be used at all.
    

But also:

- genuinely beneficial technology may be destroyed;
    
- contraband markets become lucrative;
    
- fear hardens into repression;
    
- curiosity becomes suspect;
    
- a society protected from risk may also be protected from recovery.
    

### Their relationship to the player

They may purchase dangerous artifacts for destruction or offer contracts to contain suspicious devices. They may also make it harder to openly sell weapons, unstable power sources, or complex devices. That does not necessarily reduce profit: prohibited items often become more valuable to less reputable buyers.

---

## 5. The Seekers

### What they believe

The Mountain is a question that must remain open.

They distrust simple conclusions: divine gift, commercial resource, old-world archive, existential threat. They follow anomalies, contradictions, patterns of movement, unexpected behaviors, and rumors that do not fit established theories.

Some are serious explorers. Some are visionaries. Some are cranks. Distinguishing among them is not always possible in a world where machines genuinely do impossible-seeming things.

### What they protect

- curiosity;
    
- exploration;
    
- anomaly;
    
- doubt;
    
- freedom from settled interpretation.
    

### When they gain influence

The settlement becomes adventurous and unstable:

- expeditions multiply;
    
- strange sites are investigated;
    
- rumors circulate faster;
    
- rare discoveries become more likely to be noticed;
    
- nobody accepts official explanations easily.
    

But also:

- reckless exploration rises;
    
- proven solutions are abandoned for enticing mysteries;
    
- panic spreads through rumor;
    
- unstable artifacts are pursued precisely because they are unstable.
    

### Their relationship to the player

They are interested in artifacts that contradict their apparent use. A heater that begins displaying patterns, a medical device that affects nearby machinery, or a sensor that seems to react to human voices may be far more desirable to a Seeker than a reliable, profitable object.

# How Factions Appear in Play

The player should not begin by selecting a faction badge.

Instead, the player encounters the social world the factions are creating.

A player trying to sell an artifact may discover:

- Revelationist buyers care about signs and stories;
    
- Archivist buyers care about unexpected behavior;
    
- Syndicate buyers care about resale value;
    
- Purifier agents care about containment or destruction;
    
- Seeker buyers care about anomalies.
    

The player remains an opportunist.

They may:

- sell a relic to believers;
    
- sell dangerous equipment through a black market;
    
- turn over a strange device for research;
    
- accept a destruction contract;
    
- exploit a rumor they do not believe.
    

Their repeated choices may build soft relationships and shape the settlement, but they are never required to declare an identity.

# What Faction Dominance Feels Like

Faction dominance should change the atmosphere and market context of the season, not rewrite the game rules.

## A Revelationist Season

Pilgrims crowd the approaches. Small shrines appear beside salvage heaps. People share meals more freely; they also ask more questions about what you believe. An artifact that produces clean water acquires a religious history by afternoon.

## An Archivist Season

Labels and catalogs spread across the market. Buyers ask what an artifact did before asking what it costs. More things are understood; fewer things are used quickly. Rumors of raiders beyond the perimeter are carefully documented.

## A Syndicate Season

The settlement acquires lights, signs, guards, dealers, speculators, and counterfeiters. Almost anything can be sold. Almost everyone owes someone money. A person can arrive hungry and leave rich; considerably more arrive hungry and leave indebted.

## A Purifier Season

The market becomes quiet and watchful. Inspection pits burn at night. Accidents are fewer. So are miracles. Anything unusually powerful changes hands in whispers, often for far more than it would have fetched openly.

## A Seeker Season

Everyone has a theory. Every unusual noise starts an expedition. More extraordinary discoveries are reported, though fewer reports agree with one another. The settlement feels alive with possibility and close to running off a cliff.

# The Player's Place in This World

The player is not asked to resolve the Mountain.

The player is asked to survive and profit from it.

Each day they retrieve artifacts whose purpose they do not understand. They force those artifacts into forms somebody will buy. Sometimes they push too far and destroy them. Sometimes a device briefly reveals a behavior that makes the world feel larger and stranger than it did a moment earlier.

Around them, people argue, worship, trade, document, suppress, pursue, and reinterpret.

At season's end, the Mountain disappears. The player's score is recorded. The settlement's brief history is told. The factions' influence is made visible through outcomes, not verdicts.

Then the Mountain appears somewhere else, and people begin rushing toward it again.
