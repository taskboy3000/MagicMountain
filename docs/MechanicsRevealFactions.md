# Design Policy: Make the Mechanics Reveal the Factions

Magic Mountain should not rely on exposition to communicate its world. The player should come to understand the factions primarily through repeated interaction with the game's existing mechanics.

The objective is **not** to add more lore. The objective is to make every existing system express a faction's philosophy.

Every faction represents a distinct answer to the question:

> **"What is the Mountain, and what should humanity do with it?"**

That philosophy should be visible whenever the player encounters that faction.

## Recognition over Explanation

A successful interaction is one where the player immediately recognizes who they are dealing with.

The desired player reactions are simple:

* "Great, a LibreMount buyer."
* "Ugh... the Syndicate again."
* "The Faculty is interested. I wonder why."
* "The Purifiers are going to hate this artifact."
* "The Revelationists are being weird again."

Players should develop these instincts naturally through play, not because the game explicitly explains them.

## Mechanics First, Lore Second

Mechanically, many interactions may remain identical.

A market negotiation is still a market negotiation.

A successful sale is still a successful sale.

A random event is still a random event.

What changes is **how each faction interprets the same situation.**

The writing, presentation, and flavor should communicate worldview even when the underlying game mechanics are unchanged.

## Every System Should Express Philosophy

Future polishing should review every existing system through this lens.

### Market arrivals

The first line spoken by a buyer should immediately establish their worldview.

Not just who they are.

Why they are here.

### Negotiation reactions

Matched offers, rejected offers, lowball offers, budget limits, and storm-offs should all sound like they came from different organizations with different priorities.

### Random events

Events should increasingly feel like consequences of faction activity rather than isolated random occurrences.

The world should appear to continue moving even when the player is not directly involved.

### Town Crier

The Town Crier should report ideological shifts, not simply statistical changes.

Faction dominance should feel like changes in the culture and priorities of the settlement.

### PB3K Reference Material

Documentation should read like operational field notes from the old world, occasionally hinting at how different factions interpret the same evidence.

## Preserve Mystery

The game should avoid explaining every mechanic.

Players should form theories.

They should slowly discover that certain factions consistently value certain behaviors, react to particular artifacts, or pursue recognizable goals.

Understanding should emerge from observation.

The PB3K reports what it knows.

It does not reveal the underlying equations.

## Success Criteria

When a player encounters a faction, the emotional response should come **before** the mechanical response.

Players should recognize a faction's personality before they recognize its numerical modifiers.

If two factions use identical mechanics but produce different emotional reactions, the design is succeeding.

The fiction should emerge from play, not from reading.
