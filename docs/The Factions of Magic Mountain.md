## Purpose

This document defines the first four major factions of Magic Mountain as social institutions, belief systems, and sources of narrative conflict.

It is intended to guide:

- event writing
- faction-dominant seasonal color
- NPC motivations
- market reactions to artifacts
- conflicts that arise naturally around the Mountain

It does not define:

- faction mechanics
- probability adjustments
- score effects
- player reputation formulas
- the final fifth faction

---

# Setting Assumption

The world is post-apocalyptic from the designer's perspective, but not from the perspective of most people living in it.

Whatever destroyed or transformed the old world happened long before living memory.

People do not generally think of themselves as survivors of an apocalypse. They live in an ordinary world of:

- work
- debt
- family
- trade
- religion
- status
- local feuds
- institutions
- weather
- civic irritation

Ancient machines remain scattered through the world. They are occasionally useful, frequently misunderstood, and sometimes dangerous.

The Mountain is extraordinary because it appears temporarily, releases an enormous quantity of unknown artifacts and opportunities, reshapes local life, and then disappears.

## Tone Anchor

> The catastrophe is history. The absurdity is contemporary life.

# Faction Design Principles

## Factions Are Institutions, Not Moral Alignments

No faction is the good faction.

No faction is the evil faction.

Each faction believes it understands how people should respond to the Mountain.

Each faction protects something real.

Each faction becomes ridiculous, harmful, or oppressive when its view dominates the settlement.

## Factions Do Not Need to Understand One Another Fairly

As designers, we should understand the legitimate appeal and danger of each faction.

Characters inside the world do not need to be equally generous.

They may regard rivals as:

- blasphemers
- fools
- vandals
- parasites
- frauds
- tyrants
- illiterate amateurs
- dangerous elitists

This hostility is useful. It creates event storylines without requiring artificial conflict.

## Narration Should Show Conflict, Not Lecture About It

The narration should not announce:

- "The Purifiers are fanatics."
- "The Faculty are arrogant."
- "The Syndicate are exploitative."
- "The Revelationists are irrational."

Instead, events should present small slices of behavior that allow the player to reach those conclusions.

Example:

> An inspector has marked a warm metal box for destruction. A pilgrim has placed flowers on it. The owner would prefer that either one buy it.

---

# The Central Faction Conflict

All four factions are competing to become the necessary intermediary between ordinary people and the Mountain.

| Faction | Claims Authority Through | Wants to Control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Revelationists | Divine meaning and sacred interpretation | What artifacts signify and how they may be treated |
| Purifiers | Safety, restraint, and sanctioned destruction | Which artifacts may continue to exist |
| The Faculty | Exclusive expertise and inherited knowledge | Who may study and interpret artifacts |
| The Syndicate | Markets, ownership, and distribution | Who may profit from artifacts and at what price |

The player moves among all of them as an opportunist.

The player does not need to believe any faction's doctrine in order to sell to it, work for it, offend it, or help it become powerful.

# The Revelationists

## Core Belief

The Mountain is not merely a source of useful objects.

It is a visitation.

Its appearances have meaning. Its artifacts are signs, gifts, warnings, relics, tests, or portions of a message that humanity has not yet learned to understand.

An artifact that produces clean water is not simply useful. It is evidence.

An artifact that produces incomprehensible patterns is not malfunctioning. It is communicating.

An artifact that harms people may be a warning, a judgment, or proof that it was handled without reverence.

## Institutional Personality

The Revelationists are deeply certain that the Mountain must be interpreted through faith, ritual, and moral authority.

They are not a single church with one clean doctrine. They include competing ministries, shrines, prophets, hosts, relic keepers, traveling preachers, charitable societies, frauds, schismatics, and ordinary believers.

Institutionally, they are prone to:

- converting artifacts into relics
- converting accidents into signs
- converting interpretations into doctrine
- treating practical skepticism as spiritual failure
- treating destruction of meaningful artifacts as sacrilege

They do not merely believe.

They believe other people are wrong in ways that may endanger everyone.

## What They Protect

- meaning
- hope
- ritual
- belonging
- charity
- moral order
- the conviction that life is more than trade and survival

## Their Legitimate Appeal

The Revelationists provide things people genuinely need:

- shelter for pilgrims and newcomers
- communal meals
- grief rituals
- purpose during uncertainty
- social bonds
- language for experiences ordinary life cannot explain

A settlement influenced by them may feel less lonely and less atomized.

People who have seen an artifact restore water, relieve pain, or emit impossible-seeming patterns are not fools for wondering whether it means something.

## Their Danger

The Revelationists are unable to leave meaning alone.

When influential, they may:

- prevent useful artifacts from being examined or sold
- turn coincidence into obligation
- punish doubt or irreverence
- compete violently over doctrine
- create authority from unverifiable claims
- preserve dangerous artifacts because destroying them would be sacrilege

Their certainty can convert comfort into coercion.

## Relationship to Artifacts

Revelationists especially value artifacts that:

- behave unexpectedly
- produce repeated patterns
- assist healing or water production
- appear during notable circumstances
- survive unusual failures
- attract stories

They may pay for provenance and narrative as much as practical function.

An ordinary thermal artifact found beside a collapsed shrine may be worth far more to them than a more useful device with no story attached.

## Natural Opponent: The Purifiers

The Revelationists and Purifiers are direct mirror opponents.

Both believe artifacts must not be treated casually.

Both believe the Mountain carries overwhelming significance.

Both believe ordinary prospectors are dangerously irresponsible.

But they reach opposite conclusions:

| Revelationists | Purifiers |
| --- | --- |
| The artifact may be sacred. | The artifact may be contamination. |
| Preserve it. | Destroy it. |
| Study without reverence is desecration. | Preserve without proof is recklessness. |
| Destruction erases meaning. | Reverence spreads danger. |

This conflict should be one of the most visible and productive sources of story in the game.

## View of the Other Factions

### Purifiers

Blasphemers, destroyers, frightened people who mistake the possibility of harm for a mandate to erase signs from the world.

### The Faculty

Prideful custodians who take sacred things apart, classify what they cannot understand, and confuse technical vocabulary with wisdom.

### The Syndicate

Profiteers who sell counterfeit meaning, exploit believers, and would charge admission to a miracle.

## Everyday Presence

Revelationist influence appears through:

- shrines around functioning devices
- pilgrim lodgings
- soup tents
- processions
- devotional tokens
- relic certifications
- public testimony
- doctrinal arguments
- complaints that a prospecting stall is operating too close to sacred ground

## Event Story Seeds

- A device that reliably heats a cookhouse is declared sacred after an unrelated recovery from illness.
- Purifiers arrive with tools to disable a water-producing artifact surrounded by pilgrims.
- A Revelationist buyer values an unstable artifact because it "refuses careless hands."
- Two ministries offer competing interpretations of an artifact the player is trying to sell.
- A pilgrim asks the player to conceal an artifact from inspectors, not for money, but because her family has traveled for weeks to see it.

## Writing Caution

Do not present the Revelationists as merely gullible or fraudulent.

Their danger comes from the same source as their value: they give profound meaning to a world full of events that genuinely appear miraculous.

# The Purifiers

## Core Belief

The Mountain is a recurring hazard.

The artifacts it produces are fragments of a civilization whose machines outlasted its judgment. An object does not become safe because someone can make it produce heat, light, water, medicine, or money.

The Purifiers believe that the world has suffered enough from uncontrolled technology, desperate experimentation, and people who mistake temporary usefulness for safety.

Some artifacts should not be improved.

Some artifacts should not be sold.

Some artifacts should be destroyed wherever they are found.

## Institutional Personality

The Purifiers consider restraint a form of intelligence and destruction a form of civic duty.

They establish inspections, containment protocols, destruction orders, prohibited artifact lists, safety notices, disposal pits, and authorized salvage practices.

They often believe that anyone objecting to their authority is either greedy, ignorant, corrupted by the Mountain, or insufficiently serious about public safety.

They are prone to:

- treating uncertainty as danger
- treating possession as culpability
- destroying first and justifying later
- granting themselves authority because they are willing to say no
- expanding emergency measures into permanent social control

## What They Protect

- safety
- restraint
- containment
- public health
- prevention
- responsibility
- the right not to be endangered by someone else's opportunity

## Their Legitimate Appeal

The Purifiers are not wrong that artifacts can be dangerous.

They may provide:

- fire brigades
- disposal services
- inspections
- warnings
- containment crews
- protection from reckless sellers
- intervention when poor families are being sold hazardous devices as necessities

In a settlement full of amateur engineering and desperate profit-seeking, somebody willing to ask "should this be in a kitchen?" may save lives.

## Their Danger

The Purifiers turn fear into authority.

When influential, they may:

- destroy useful devices
- suppress experimentation
- create contraband markets
- prohibit artifacts based on suspicion rather than evidence
- treat ordinary people as incapable of judgment
- allow safety to become an excuse for censorship, confiscation, and control

Their certainty can convert caution into repression.

## Relationship to Artifacts

Purifiers especially attend to artifacts that:

- produce weapons-like force
- emit unexplained energy
- alter living tissue
- generate unstable output
- cause illness, fire, or unusual behavior
- attract unsupervised public use

They may buy artifacts for surrender, inspection, containment, or destruction.

An artifact can be valuable to them precisely because they want no one else to have it.

## Natural Opponent: The Revelationists

The Purifiers see Revelationists as their most dangerous opponents because Revelationists do not merely use risky artifacts. They give those artifacts protected moral status.

From a Purifier perspective, a dangerous machine in a merchant stall can be confiscated.

A dangerous machine surrounded by grieving families, pilgrims, and people calling it a blessing is far harder to remove.

## Secondary Opponents

### The Faculty

The Purifiers regard the Faculty as arrogant collectors who preserve hazards in locked rooms and mistake observation for responsibility.

Their criticism is simple:

> A well-labeled catastrophe is still a catastrophe.

### The Syndicate

The Purifiers regard the Syndicate as professional distributors of harm: people who will sell an unstable device to a family, then sell disposal services after it burns down the kitchen.

## View of the Other Factions

### Revelationists

Fanatics granting sacred immunity to dangerous technology.

### The Faculty

Credentialed cowards who will catalog a threat until it kills someone outside their walls.

### The Syndicate

Dealers whose entire business model depends on somebody else bearing the risk.

## Everyday Presence

Purifier influence appears through:

- inspection tents
- warning signs
- seizure notices
- containment wagons
- public destruction pits
- permit queues
- restricted sales
- sealed stalls
- quiet nighttime contraband
- families arguing about whether a useful household device is safe enough to keep

## Event Story Seeds

- A cookhouse loses its heating artifact after several customers develop the same tremor.
- A proscribed device becomes more profitable immediately after Purifiers issue a warning about it.
- Purifiers ask the player to surrender an artifact that a family claims is keeping an elderly relative alive.
- A destruction pit produces steady heat, and nearby vendors begin using it for cooking.
- An inspector asks whether an artifact ever produced light "without being instructed to do so."

## Writing Caution

Do not present the Purifiers as simple enemies of progress.

Their danger is meaningful only because they are often right that other people are taking unacceptable risks.

# The Faculty

## Common External Name

Archivists

## Preferred Internal Name

The Faculty

## Core Belief

The Mountain is evidence.

The greatest threat is not faith, fear, or greed alone. It is ignorance: people acting upon artifacts without the education, records, methods, or discipline required to understand what they are seeing.

The Faculty believes that artifacts must be recovered, cataloged, preserved, compared, and studied by qualified custodians.

Conveniently, the Faculty also believes that it is the only institution qualified to decide who is qualified.

## Institutional Personality

The Faculty is a hereditary imitation of a university system reconstructed from fragments, myths, inherited practices, and naked social privilege.

It puts the cult back in faculty.

It contains:

- inherited chairs
- formal robes and insignia
- collections and restricted stacks
- examinations of questionable relevance
- citation customs
- disciplinary committees
- rival schools of interpretation
- honorary titles sold to patrons
- family lines claiming descent from founding scholars
- warehouses full of artifacts awaiting study
- public lectures whose primary function is demonstrating status

The Faculty does not merely hoard artifacts.

It hoards the right to say what counts as knowledge.

Its members genuinely believe that ordinary people, priests, regulators, and merchants are too ignorant to be trusted with the Mountain.

Often they have evidence supporting that belief.

## What They Protect

- knowledge
- memory
- records
- preservation
- disciplined observation
- technical continuity
- resistance to superstition and panic

## Their Legitimate Appeal

The Faculty may genuinely be the organization most likely to recognize:

- repeated artifact behaviors
- health hazards
- practical improvements
- correlations across seasons
- useful applications overlooked by buyers
- false claims circulating through the market

A functioning archive can prevent every generation from repeating the same catastrophic mistakes.

An artifact destroyed, worshipped, or immediately sold may represent an irreplaceable lost opportunity for understanding.

## Their Danger

The Faculty does not merely value knowledge. It converts knowledge into caste.

When influential, it may:

- restrict public access to artifacts
- preserve useful objects while ordinary people need them
- refuse to share discoveries outside approved circles
- treat practical folk knowledge as beneath notice
- define dissent as ignorance
- protect inherited authority through opaque standards
- consider the public unworthy of information recovered from public risk

Their society may become more knowledgeable while becoming less free, less equal, and less capable of applying knowledge to ordinary suffering.

## Relationship to Artifacts

The Faculty especially values artifacts that:

- show unexpected secondary behavior
- generate patterns
- reveal contradictions
- collapse in unusual ways
- appear related to previously cataloged objects
- cannot be reduced to an immediate practical use

A reliable heater is useful.

A heater that begins displaying repeating structures is a specimen.

A broken artifact with careful observations may be worth more to the Faculty than a functioning artifact with no documented history.

## Natural Opponents: Revelationists and Purifiers

The Faculty regards both Revelationists and Purifiers as enemies of knowledge.

### Revelationists

The Faculty believes Revelationists:

- contaminate evidence with stories
- hide artifacts inside sacred practice
- confuse emotional significance with observation
- make investigation politically dangerous
- transform technical ignorance into mass certainty

To the Faculty, a shrine is an uncontrolled storage site run by people incapable of keeping records.

### Purifiers

The Faculty believes Purifiers:

- destroy unique evidence
- mistake fear for method
- erase what they cannot explain
- call censorship safety
- make future understanding impossible

To the Faculty, a destruction pit is a library fire staffed by people proud of their work.

## Relationship to the Syndicate

The Faculty despises the Syndicate but frequently relies upon it.

The Syndicate:

- finds specimens
- finances expeditions
- provides storage
- moves goods
- procures restricted artifacts
- purchases practical applications of Faculty research

Faculty members may publicly condemn commercial vulgarity while privately competing for patrons, acquisition budgets, and exclusive contracts.

The Syndicate finds this arrangement amusing.

## View of the Other Factions

### Revelationists

Illiterate mystics assigning holidays to devices before recording their output.

### Purifiers

Destructive zealots whose preferred research method is fire.

### The Syndicate

Vulgar middlemen, traffickers, and patrons whose money remains regrettably useful.

## Everyday Presence

Faculty influence appears through:

- catalog tents
- restricted collections
- lectures
- examinations
- storage depots
- accession forms
- technical corrections posted publicly
- scholars disputing terminology
- families attempting to reclaim artifacts donated by ancestors
- ordinary prospectors being told their own observations are improperly documented

## Event Story Seeds

- A Faculty buyer refuses to purchase an unusual artifact because the player cannot state where it was found to within three paces.
- A useful heating device sits in a locked collection while a nearby school closes during cold weather.
- A junior Faculty member quietly offers money for an artifact her chair has officially declared uninteresting.
- Revelationists declare a device sacred after the Faculty tries to remove it for study.
- Purifiers destroy a dangerous artifact seconds before a Faculty representative arrives with a court-like injunction written in a dead technical vocabulary.
- The Faculty publishes a correction changing an artifact classification from "possibly responsive" to "responsively possible."

## Writing Caution

Do not present the Faculty as the rational faction.

They may understand more than anyone else. Their central corruption is believing that understanding gives them ownership of the world.

# The Syndicate

## Core Belief

The Mountain is opportunity.

People can argue about purpose, danger, revelation, or knowledge after they have eaten, found shelter, moved their goods, financed their next expedition, and sold what they brought back.

The Syndicate does not need to know what the Mountain means.

It needs to know:

- what people need
- what people fear
- what people will pay for
- what they cannot legally buy
- what explanation improves the price

## Institutional Personality

The Syndicate is less a single organized faction than a dense commercial culture surrounding the Mountain.

It includes:

- traders
- moneylenders
- haulers
- guards
- brokers
- appraisers
- innkeepers
- cooks
- bookmakers
- counterfeiters
- insurers
- stall owners
- debt collectors
- artifact speculators
- people who insist they are ordinary business owners and resent the word Syndicate

The Syndicate has no interest in treating rival doctrines fairly.

Its instinct is to convert them into demand.

To a Revelationist, it sells relics.

To a Purifier, it sells compliant disposal, secure transport, and discreet access to things that should not be available.

To the Faculty, it sells specimens, storage, patrons, and plausible provenance.

The Syndicate is happy to parrot any belief as long as someone is paying to hear it.

## What They Protect

- exchange
- distribution
- enterprise
- mobility
- material comfort
- liquidity
- practical access to goods
- individual advancement

## Their Legitimate Appeal

On rare reflective days, after the scrip has been counted, Syndicate members may recognize that they serve a genuine purpose.

They can plausibly say:

> Priests do not haul grain. Inspectors do not manufacture blankets. Faculty chairs do not unload water barrels. Somebody has to move things from people who have them to people who need them.

A Syndicate-influenced settlement may be:

- well supplied
- active
- easier to enter and survive in
- full of work
- full of services
- open to people with nerve and practical ability

Markets are not an illusion. Distribution matters in a hard world.

## Their Danger

The Syndicate is incapable of seeing a boundary that cannot become a price.

When influential, it may:

- exploit scarcity
- finance desperation
- create debt dependency
- commercialize faith
- create black markets around safety rules
- fund exclusive Faculty access
- turn basic necessities into speculative goods
- make poverty both more survivable and more permanent

The Syndicate does not need to hate people to exploit them. It only needs to accurately understand their leverage.

## Relationship to Artifacts

The Syndicate values whatever can be sold.

It is especially interested in artifacts that are:

- immediately useful
- scarce in the current environment
- easy to demonstrate
- prohibited and therefore lucrative
- story-rich enough to sell at a premium
- mysterious enough that buyers can project value onto them

A stable heater is merchandise.

An unstable heater is specialty equipment.

A prohibited heater is an opportunity.

A sacred heater is an opportunity with a story.

## Relationship to the Other Factions

The Syndicate does not respect the views of the other factions, but it respects their ability to create markets.

### Revelationists

Useful producers of pilgrimage, relic markets, donations, sacred branding, lodging demand, and customers willing to pay for provenance.

The Syndicate considers Revelationist doctrine nonsense, but profitable nonsense deserves professional handling.

### Purifiers

A regulatory burden and an excellent source of scarcity.

Purifier prohibitions complicate business, but they also create premium markets, disposal contracts, licensing fees, security work, and customers willing to pay for discretion.

### The Faculty

Insufferable elitists who require warehouses, expedition financing, equipment, donors, private transportation, and sellers willing to acquire artifacts without excessive questions.

The Syndicate considers Faculty disdain a luxury purchased with Syndicate services.

## View of the Other Factions

### Revelationists

Customers with unusually elaborate product requirements.

### Purifiers

Inspectors who make some products harder to sell and therefore worth more.

### The Faculty

Buyers who require paperwork in order to pretend they are above buying.

## Everyday Presence

Syndicate influence appears through:

- expanded markets
- auction notices
- new lodging
- food stalls
- gambling
- private security
- price boards
- artifact insurance
- counterfeit relics
- debt arrangements
- premium access lanes
- vendors selling solutions to inconveniences introduced by other vendors

## Event Story Seeds

- A trader offers the player three possible sales descriptions for an artifact, each intended for a different faction.
- Pilgrims arrive to find devotional lodging, souvenir stalls, and a mandatory suggested donation for water.
- A prohibited artifact becomes the subject of a quiet auction attended by two Purifier officials in civilian clothes.
- A Faculty patron publicly condemns artifact trafficking while bidding through an intermediary.
- A lender offers equipment financing at excellent terms that become less excellent in smaller print.
- The settlement's first public drinking fountain is immediately enclosed by an awning bearing the name of its sponsor.

## Writing Caution

Do not present the Syndicate as merely criminal or heartless.

Its members should often be the people making the settlement function. The horror and humor come from the fact that usefulness, prosperity, and exploitation are not separate systems for them.

# Primary Conflict Map

## Revelationists versus Purifiers

This is the most direct factional opposition.

They are competing zealots with opposite imperatives:

- preserve and venerate
- contain and destroy

Their conflicts should frequently place ordinary people between sacred value and physical danger.

## The Faculty versus Revelationists

This is a conflict over interpretation and access.

- Revelationists believe meaning belongs to the community of believers.
- The Faculty believes interpretation belongs to qualified custodians.

Each regards the other as an institution built around ignorance.

## The Faculty versus Purifiers

This is a conflict over evidence and danger.

- Purifiers believe uncertain hazards should be eliminated.
- The Faculty believes destroyed evidence makes future safety impossible.

Each regards the other as recklessly endangering humanity.

## The Syndicate versus Everyone

The Syndicate is not an ideological opponent in the same way.

It is a corrosive intermediary.

It takes each faction's convictions and turns them into:

- buyers
- shortages
- contracts
- premium services
- black markets
- advertisements
- investment opportunities

Every faction condemns this behavior while eventually requiring some Syndicate service.

# Faction-Dominant Seasonal Color

A dominant faction does not replace the settlement with a single doctrine.

It shifts what becomes ordinary, respectable, profitable, suspicious, or unavoidable.

## Revelationist-Dominant Season

The settlement gains:

- pilgrims
- shrines
- charitable kitchens
- testimonies
- relic markets
- public rituals
- doctrinal quarrels

Ordinary inconvenience:

- practical devices become socially difficult to use because someone has declared them sacred.

## Purifier-Dominant Season

The settlement gains:

- inspections
- warnings
- confiscation procedures
- safety services
- disposal pits
- sealed stalls
- quiet contraband

Ordinary inconvenience:

- useful devices become difficult to keep, repair, or sell without official attention.

## Faculty-Dominant Season

The settlement gains:

- catalogs
- collections
- lectures
- restricted access
- committees
- preservation warehouses
- credential disputes

Ordinary inconvenience:

- everyone knows that useful artifacts exist, but fewer people are permitted to use them.

## Syndicate-Dominant Season

The settlement gains:

- stalls
- transport
- food
- security
- auctions
- betting
- lending
- advertisements
- counterfeit goods

Ordinary inconvenience:

- every ordinary need becomes an opportunity for someone else to charge a fee.

# Player-Facing Narrative Rule

Faction conflict should normally be encountered as an immediate practical situation.

The player should meet:

- a buyer
- an inspector
- a pilgrim
- a junior scholar
- a lender
- a desperate family
- a sealed stall
- a shrine
- a destroyed artifact
- a prohibited artifact offered at a higher price

The player should not usually receive a speech explaining faction doctrine.

## Strong Event Pattern

1. An artifact has an observable use or problem.
2. A faction gives that use or problem a particular meaning.
3. Another faction, or the player's own interest, conflicts with that meaning.
4. The player sees an opportunity, a risk, or an inconvenience.
5. The narration does not explain the moral conclusion.

Example:

> The box produces enough heat to keep a room warm. A Revelationist family has placed candles around it. A Purifier inspector has attached a destruction notice to the table. The family asks whether you know how to remove paper without touching the box.

# #LibreMount

## Core Belief

The Mountain belongs to nobody.

No priest may declare its artifacts sacred property.

No inspector may destroy what another person might use.

No Faculty heir may hide its devices behind locked doors and inherited credentials.

No trader may fence off access to the Mountain and charge ordinary people for the privilege of receiving what was never theirs to own.

LibreMount believes that the moment any person or institution claims the authority to manage the Mountain for others, the Mountain has been stolen.

They do not argue that authority sometimes becomes corrupt.

They argue that authority is the corruption.

## Institutional Personality

LibreMount is a militant anti-custody movement.

It includes:

- access-route cutters
- unlicensed prospectors
- artifact liberators
- warehouse raiders
- anti-toll organizers
- public distributors
- saboteurs
- angry families
- practical anarchists
- dispossessed workers
- people whose useful devices were confiscated, priced beyond reach, locked away, or turned into holy objects
- idealists who sincerely believe that ordinary people should be free to take their own chances

LibreMount is not fundamentally a charity.

Its members may distribute artifacts among poor families, open a warehouse during a cold spell, or remove a useful machine from a locked Faculty collection. But they do these things because they oppose custody, not because they accept responsibility for the people receiving the artifacts.

A LibreMount activist may risk arrest to break open an impound yard full of confiscated devices.

The same activist may refuse to establish a reliable public fire brigade, because any group empowered to remove hazards from private homes is already becoming another Purifier authority.

They are generous when generosity breaks a gate.

They are less reliable when help requires building something that lasts.

## What They Protect

- personal autonomy
- unmediated access
- freedom from institutional custody
- unlicensed use
- the right to take risks without permission
- resistance to class privilege
- resistance to enclosure
- the destruction of gatekeeping systems

## Their Legitimate Appeal

LibreMount exists because every other faction eventually tells ordinary people that the Mountain does not belong to them.

The Revelationists may turn useful artifacts into sacred objects that cannot be touched.

The Purifiers may confiscate or destroy devices that families need.

The Faculty may lock valuable objects away for generations of study accessible only to its own caste.

The Syndicate may acquire access routes, storage yards, water devices, heating equipment, and practical tools, then sell their use back to the people who helped recover them.

LibreMount can truthfully say:

> Everyone claims to be protecting you from the Mountain. Notice that they all end up holding the key.

When LibreMount is useful, it may:

- open access routes controlled by private guards
- disrupt predatory tolls
- return useful artifacts to ordinary households
- expose Faculty hoarding
- prevent Purifiers from destroying genuinely beneficial devices
- remove practical equipment from shrines where it has ceased to serve anyone
- teach prospectors how to avoid brokers and institutional buyers
- break monopolies during periods of scarcity

A family freezing beside a locked warehouse may have very little patience for lectures about stewardship.

A settlement where every useful thing has already been claimed may find LibreMount extremely persuasive.

## Their Danger

LibreMount cannot distinguish authority from stewardship.

It distrusts not only corrupt institutions, but any durable institution capable of setting rules, maintaining standards, preserving scarce resources, or accepting responsibility for public welfare.

When influential, LibreMount may:

- distribute unstable artifacts without testing them
- remove warning seals from dangerous devices
- dismantle valuable complex artifacts for immediate household uses
- open shared stores before the next emergency arrives
- sabotage markets that were reliably moving needed goods
- reject expertise as disguised class rule
- prevent inspection even when inspection would save lives
- denounce organized public services as the early stages of tyranny
- turn angry crowds against anyone accused of hoarding, regulating, preserving, or profiting

Their ideal society is not one where everyone is cared for.

It is one where no one has sufficient authority to compel, coordinate, restrict, preserve, or protect.

Their certainty can convert liberation into helplessness.

## Relationship to Artifacts

LibreMount values artifacts as things ordinary people should be permitted to use without institutional approval.

They are especially interested in artifacts that are:

- practically useful in households or public spaces
- held in Faculty collections
- confiscated by Purifiers
- controlled by Syndicate markets or warehouses
- installed in Revelationist shrines despite obvious material usefulness
- accompanied by access restrictions, permits, prices, or claims of exclusive custody

LibreMount is less interested in whether an artifact is fully understood than whether someone is being denied its possible use.

Examples:

- A heating artifact locked in a Faculty collection should be placed in a cold tenement.
- A water-producing device surrounded by a Revelationist shrine should be connected to a public cistern.
- A proscribed medical-seeming device should be returned to the family who believes it was helping their child.
- A Syndicate warehouse full of stable household artifacts should be opened before anyone can attach prices to them.

LibreMount may be correct about the immediate injustice.

It may also be distributing an artifact whose consequences nobody has had time to observe.

## Natural Opponent: The Syndicate

LibreMount and the Syndicate are natural enemies because they disagree about whether ownership creates value or destroys freedom.

The Syndicate believes artifacts become useful when people can own, improve, transport, price, finance, insure, and sell them.

LibreMount believes ownership is simply gatekeeping with paperwork.

| The Syndicate | LibreMount |
| --- | --- |
| Price distributes scarce goods. | Price keeps common wealth from people without money. |
| Ownership rewards risk and investment. | Ownership rewards whoever fenced the opportunity first. |
| Warehouses preserve useful stock. | Warehouses prove someone is withholding access. |
| Tolls fund safer routes and services. | Tolls turn freedom of movement into permission. |
| Markets give people choices. | Markets give wealthy people first choice. |

The Syndicate may initially find LibreMount useful. Both dislike Faculty privilege, Purifier interference, and Revelationist custody of practical artifacts.

That understanding ends as soon as LibreMount opens a warehouse, interrupts an auction, cuts a toll chain, or informs customers that payment is optional.

## Natural Opponent: The Faculty

LibreMount regards the Faculty as a hereditary ruling class that has constructed an elaborate justification for keeping the Mountain away from ordinary people.

From the LibreMount point of view, the Faculty does not preserve knowledge.

It seizes objects recovered at public risk, locks them in private collections, writes inaccessible descriptions of them, and declares everyone else unqualified to ask for them back.

The Faculty regards LibreMount as organized vandalism: people who destroy provenance, scatter irreplaceable material, corrupt evidence, and place dangerous systems into the hands of people who cannot recognize their hazards.

Both accusations have force.

A Faculty vault may contain a device capable of improving life for hundreds of people.

Opening the vault may also distribute dozens of artifacts whose risks were the reason the door was locked.

## Relationship to the Purifiers

LibreMount regards Purifiers as authorities who destroy public inheritance in the name of protecting people from their own choices.

To LibreMount:

- a warning seal is a claim of ownership
- a destruction order is confiscation followed by irreversible waste
- an inspection requirement is permission granted by people who never had the right to grant it

Purifiers regard LibreMount as the nightmare that justifies their existence: activists removing safeguards, handing unstable devices to families, and treating every preventable accident as an acceptable cost of liberty.

Their conflict becomes most intense when an artifact is both useful and visibly dangerous.

## Relationship to the Revelationists

LibreMount may have more sympathy for ordinary Revelationist believers than for Faculty custodians or Syndicate brokers. But it rejects the idea that spiritual meaning can remove an artifact from common use.

To LibreMount, converting a practical artifact into a shrine is another form of enclosure.

A heat source that could warm a public dormitory is still being withheld if it sits beneath ribbons and candles.

A water device is still being monopolized if access depends on reverence.

Revelationists regard LibreMount as profaners: people so incapable of gratitude that they dismantle signs and gifts into household appliances.

LibreMount regards Revelationists as people who place sacred language around useful things and then appoint themselves custodians.

## Relationship to Ordinary People

LibreMount's most important conflict is not with another faction.

It is with ordinary people who sometimes want institutions.

People may want:

- a heater certified as safe before placing it near their children
- a clinic that keeps records
- a merchant who can be held to a warranty
- a fire crew empowered to remove dangerous equipment
- a storehouse protected until winter
- a shrine preserved because it means something to their family
- a scholar to examine a device before it is distributed
- someone, somewhere, to be responsible when things go wrong

LibreMount may regard these desires as surrender.

It may free people from systems they were angry about, only to discover that they depended upon some of those systems.

It may not consider that discovery especially relevant.

## View of the Other Factions

### Revelationists

Custodians in ceremonial clothing, declaring useful objects sacred so ordinary people will accept being denied them.

### Purifiers

Fear merchants who destroy what they cannot control and call it public responsibility.

### The Faculty

Inherited parasites hiding public wealth behind locks, robes, catalogs, and condescension.

### The Syndicate

Fence-builders who take what no one made, attach a price, and call the transaction freedom.

### Ordinary Citizens Who Object

People taught to beg for permission when they should be cutting locks.

## Everyday Presence

LibreMount influence appears through:

- cut fences
- removed warning seals
- open access lanes
- free artifact tables
- public notices identifying alleged hoarders
- demonstrations outside Faculty collections
- interrupted auctions
- communal workshops with dubious safety practices
- toll stations pulled apart overnight
- impound carts found empty
- household devices bearing scratched-off inventory numbers
- market stalls refusing to display ownership marks
- arguments over whether storing supplies is prudent planning or enclosure

LibreMount should feel active, confrontational, practical, and difficult to ignore.

Its members do not merely complain about authority.

They remove it where they find it.

## When LibreMount Becomes Dominant

The settlement becomes more open, more improvised, more equal in access, and much harder to coordinate.

Possible benefits:

- ordinary people gain access to practical artifacts
- expensive tolls and monopolies collapse
- useful devices circulate widely
- Faculty collections are opened
- Purifier confiscations become difficult to enforce
- shrines cannot easily keep practical artifacts from public use
- poor households receive equipment that would otherwise be priced beyond reach
- prospectors operate with fewer institutional obstacles

Possible costs:

- dangerous artifacts circulate freely
- warnings are ignored because someone issued them
- medical and household devices are used without accountability
- long-term storage becomes politically suspicious
- useful markets become unreliable
- public works fail because durable coordination resembles authority
- rare artifacts are dismantled for immediate use
- mobs punish alleged gatekeepers without proving harm
- nobody can organize a response to serious danger without being accused of becoming the next ruling class

A LibreMount-dominant settlement is not a utopia of mutual aid.

It is a place where no one can easily prevent another person from taking a chance, and where fewer people are willing or permitted to take responsibility for the consequences.

## Event Story Seeds

- LibreMount activists open a Syndicate warehouse during a cold spell and distribute heating artifacts through the neighborhood. By evening, three households report the same smell.
- A Faculty collection is broken open. A crowd takes whatever it can carry. A junior scholar offers the player money to retrieve one unremarkable metal case before anyone tries to activate it.
- A family thanks LibreMount for removing a Purifier seal from its water device. Their neighbors ask whether the dead insects beneath the runoff pipe were already there.
- A shrine's central artifact disappears overnight and reappears in a communal kitchen. Meals improve immediately. Pilgrims begin naming suspects.
- A LibreMount organizer asks the player to help dismantle a toll barrier leading to the lower slopes. The Syndicate guard claims the toll paid for clearing collapses from the path.
- A public emergency storehouse is condemned as a monopoly before it contains anything.
- LibreMount volunteers distribute a printed guide for converting signal-producing panels into cooking timers. The guide is very popular and only occasionally accurate.
- A merchant whose inventory was distributed for free offers the player payment to retrieve whatever remains. Several recipients have already sold their devices.
- A volunteer refuses to help reinforce a collapsing walkway because doing so would establish control over passage. He does offer tools for removing the nearby permit booth.

## Writing Caution

Do not present LibreMount as the faction of kindness or communal harmony.

Its members may perform brave, generous, and genuinely liberating acts. But their defining loyalty is not to welfare. It is to freedom from custody.

Do not make them foolish simply because they distrust authority. Other factions genuinely do hoard, restrict, exploit, destroy, and sanctify artifacts at ordinary people's expense.

Their danger comes from the fact that they are often right about abuses of authority and therefore become incapable of recognizing legitimate responsibility.

# Final Tone Rules

- The world is old, lived-in, and ordinary to its inhabitants.
- The Mountain makes normal ambition behave strangely.
- Each faction protects something real.
- Each faction produces benefits and harms when influential.
- No faction knows the truth of the Mountain.
- No faction should be reduced to a joke.
- Humor comes from people taking their circumstances seriously.
- The player is an opportunist moving among these forces, not a destined champion of one of them.
